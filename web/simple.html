How the Meaning of "Freedom" Changed Over Time

We analyzed millions of texts from the past 500 years to see how people have used the word "freedom" differently across history.

The Big Picture

Has the word "freedom" always meant the same thing?

To find out, we looked at how "freedom" was used in books, newspapers, and other texts going back to the year 1500. We tracked which words appeared near "freedom" and how those nearby words changed over time.

Key Finding: Liberty Labels Change as Proportions, Not as a Flip
Period Positive-liberty share Negative-liberty share
1800s17%83%
1950s-1970s25-27%73-75%
1990s-2000s28-29%71-72%
2020s21%79%

The active hypothesis is now a trend claim: the share of capacity/empowerment uses changes over time among sentence-level liberty claims.

There is no single tipping-point claim here. Negative-liberty uses remain the larger class in the current Hansard labels, while positive-liberty uses rise and fall as a changing proportion.

But here's the catch: Modern positive freedoms increasingly imply trade-offs. "Financial freedom" (living off investments) relies on the broader economy's labor to generate returns. "Medical freedom" (often framed as the right to refuse treatment) can conflict with public health goals like herd immunity. If my freedom is perceived as a cost to you, we are no longer sharing a value—we are negotiating a conflict.

Why We Dropped the Grammar Shortcut

Earlier versions tried to classify liberty with surface phrase patterns. That was too brittle: the same policy sentence can describe non-interference or enabled capacity depending on the argument being made.

Current measurement: each sentence is classified semantically as negative liberty, positive liberty, ambiguous, or other.

The trend analysis then asks whether the proportions of those labels change by decade. Grammar is no longer used as an active measurement method.

What Else We Discovered

  • "Freedom" and "liberty" are drifting apart. They used to mean almost the same thing. Now they're used in different ways. [HistWords: 1800-1990; COHA: 1820-2010]
  • "Financial freedom" is booming. This phrase grew 8.8x more common between the 1970s and 2010s. [Google Ngrams: 1800-2019]
  • "Personal freedom" and "political freedom" peaked in 1971 and have been declining ever since. [Google Ngrams: 1800-2019]
  • In the 1500s-1700s, "God" was the #1 word appearing near "freedom." Today, religious associations are much weaker. [EEBO-TCP: 1500-1700]
  • The 2020s fragmented "freedom" into competing meanings. Medical freedom, financial freedom, freedom of speech—different groups claim the word differently. [Google Trends: 2004-2025]

What We Measured

We looked at two main things:

  1. Nearby words: Which words tend to appear close to "freedom" in a sentence? For example, in the 1800s, words like "unrestrained" and "enjoyment" appeared nearby. By the 1990s, words like "democracy" and "autonomy" were more common.
  2. Phrase frequency: How often do specific phrases like "financial freedom" or "political freedom" appear in books?

Important limitation: We don't have good data for 1700-1800. This is the period of the American and French Revolutions—a crucial time for freedom discourse that we can't fully analyze.

Where the Word Came From

The word "freedom" has ancient roots:

Ancient Times

The original root word meant "to love." It evolved to mean "beloved ones"—members of your own family or tribe, as opposed to slaves who were outsiders.

450-1100 (Old English)

"Frēodōm" meant simply: not being a slave. It was a legal status—you were either free or you weren't. There was no middle ground.

1100-1500 (Middle English)

The meaning expanded to include citizenship rights, guild membership, and spiritual liberty.

"That is freedom, that a man may do what he will." — Old English text, around 900 CE

Key insight: Originally, freedom was defined by what it wasn't—slavery. This opposition between freedom and slavery has remained central to the word's meaning for over 1,500 years.

Words Getting Closer to "Freedom"

One of the biggest changes: "autonomy" moved much closer to "freedom."

In 1800, "autonomy" was rarely used near "freedom." By 1990, they were closely linked. This shows freedom becoming more about individual self-determination.

The FIRE Movement

The rise of "financial freedom" tracks with specific cultural events:

1992

Your Money or Your Life published—the book that started the Financial Independence movement.

1997

Rich Dad Poor Dad made "financial freedom" a mainstream aspiration.

2011

Mr. Money Mustache blog brought "FIRE" (Financial Independence, Retire Early) to a wider audience.

2018

"Playing with FIRE" documentary—the movement enters popular culture.

Two kinds of "economic freedom":

  • "Financial freedom" (personal) = freedom FROM debt, ability to retire early. Growing fast.
  • "Economic freedom" (political) = free markets, capitalism vs. communism. Stagnant since Cold War ended.

A Curious Finding: Search vs. Books

We found something interesting when comparing what people write in books vs. what they search for online:

Term In Books In Google Searches
personal freedom Declining since 1971 Rising
political freedom Declining since 1971 Rising
financial freedom Rising fast Peaked in 2007, flat since

What might this mean? People may search for things they feel are threatened or declining. "Personal freedom" appears less in books, but people search for it more—perhaps because they feel it's under threat.

Summary: How "Freedom" Changed

Before 1500

Legal status. Freedom meant one thing: not being a slave. It was concrete and binary. [Historical dictionaries]

1500-1700

Religious meaning. "God" was the most common word near "freedom," and early modern contexts are dominated by theological and conscience-related uses. [EEBO-TCP]

1800-1920

Legal and political pressure. Abolition, reform, empire, and parliamentary rights debates made liberty language central to institutional conflict. [Hansard, HistWords]

1950s-2000s

Changing proportions. Positive-liberty labels become a larger share of substantive liberty claims, peaking around the late 20th century in the current Hansard labels. [LLM sentence labels]

1971-2019

Financial turn. Political/personal freedom declined while "financial freedom" surged 8.8x. Freedom became about individual entitlements. [Google Ngrams]

~1880

Conceptual expansion. New Liberal arguments around capacity, welfare, and self-development offer historical context for later positive-liberty language, but the computational claim is about changing proportions rather than a hard break. [HistWords + historical interpretation]

2020-present

Fragmentation. Multiple competing "freedoms" (medical, financial, religious, speech). When freedom means "capacity," your taxes fund my freedom—and disagreement is inevitable. [Google Trends]

What We Couldn't Measure

Our analysis has some important gaps:

  • 1700-1800 gap: We don't have good data for the period of the American and French Revolutions.
  • American focus: Most of our data is from American English. British usage might be different.
  • Published text bias: We analyzed books and newspapers, not everyday speech.
  • No political breakdown: We didn't separate how conservatives vs. progressives use "freedom."
