# How "Freedom" Changed Its Meaning

We analyzed 500 years of English texts to track how people use the word "freedom." What we found surprised us.

---

## The Big Discovery

Freedom hasn't slowly evolved. It has **swung back and forth**.

| Period | "Freedom FROM" | "Freedom TO" |
|--------|----------------|--------------|
| 1500-1700 | 21% | **79%** |
| 1870-1920 | **76%** | 24% |
| 2010s | 35% | **65%** |

**In the 1500s**, freedom was mostly about what you could DO: "liberty to speak," "freedom to worship."

**By 1900**, abolition had flipped this. Freedom became about escaping constraints: "freedom from slavery," "freedom from oppression."

**In 1965**, the lines crossed again. Today we're back to positive framing—but with complex new content. Terms like "financial freedom" focus on capacity (what you can *do*), while others, like "medical freedom," use positive language ("freedom to choose") to achieve negative goals ("freedom from mandates").

### The Catch

Modern positive freedoms increasingly imply a trade-off.

- **"Financial freedom"** (living off investments) relies on the broader economy's labor to generate returns.
- **"Medical freedom"** (often framed as the right to refuse treatment) can conflict with public health goals like herd immunity.

This helps explain why "freedom" has become such a contested word. If my freedom is perceived as a cost to you, we are no longer sharing a value—we are negotiating a conflict.

---

## This Isn't Just Grammar

You might wonder: maybe "freedom from sin" and "freedom to worship" mean the same thing, just phrased differently?

We tested this by looking at what comes after "FROM" and "TO":

| What follows "FROM" | What follows "TO" |
|---------------------|-------------------|
| 61% religious concepts (sin, condemnation) | **82% action verbs** (do, speak, worship) |
| 18% constraints (persecution, punishment) | 12% religious concepts (preach, serve) |
| 9% feelings (fear, pain) | 7% choices (choose, live) |

**Only 18% overlap.** These are genuinely different categories.

- **"Freedom FROM"** names things to be removed (sin, fear, slavery)
- **"Freedom TO"** names things you can do (speak, worship, choose)

The historical shifts we found are real changes in meaning, not just different ways of saying the same thing.

## Where "Freedom" Started

### Ancient Roots

The word comes from *prī-*, meaning "to love." It evolved to mean "beloved ones"—your family or tribe, as opposed to slaves who were outsiders.

### Old English (450-1100)

**"Frēodōm"** meant one thing: not being a slave.

> "That is freedom, that a man may do what he will."
> — Old English text, around 900 CE

Freedom was:
- **Concrete**: A legal status, not an abstract idea
- **Binary**: You were either free or a slave—no middle ground
- **Defined by its opposite**: Freedom meant not-slavery

What it didn't include: freedom of thought, economic freedom, or freedom as philosophy.

## The Religious Era (1500-1700)

We analyzed over 53,000 texts from this period.

### The #1 Word Near "Freedom": God

From 1550 to 1700, "God" was the most common word appearing near "freedom" in English texts. The dominant patterns were:

- **"Christian liberty"**: Freedom from Old Testament laws
- **"God grants freedom"**: God as the source of liberty
- **"Liberty of conscience"**: Freedom to worship as you choose
- **"Freedom from sin"**: Spiritual liberation

### Why 79% Positive?

In this period, freedom was mostly religious: freedom TO worship, liberty TO preach. The negative framing ("freedom from sin") existed but was secondary.

### The Bridge to Modern Freedom

One phrase was crucial: **"liberty of conscience."**

It started as a spiritual idea (freedom of your soul) but became a political demand (freedom to worship without government interference).

> "Liberty of Conscience is every man's natural Right."
> — John Locke, 1689

*(Note: Our data has a gap during the 1700s, meaning we miss the specific phrasing of the American and French Revolutions. However, when the data resumes in the 19th century, the focus has shifted entirely.)*

---

## The Abolition Shift (1800-1920)

### The Turning Point: 1820-1830

This decade shows the biggest semantic shift in our data. It coincides with:
- British abolition debates
- The American anti-slavery movement

### Why the Shift to Negative?

Abolition centered on what people were freed FROM: slavery, bondage, chains. This wasn't abstract—it was concrete liberation from a specific condition.

By 1870-1920, **76% of freedom language** was about escaping constraints:
- "Freedom from slavery"
- "Freedom from want"
- "Freedom from fear"

## The Financial Turn (1970-Today)

### Two Paths Diverge

After 1971, something changed:

| Phrase | Change | Peak Year |
|--------|--------|-----------|
| "debt freedom" | 58.7x growth | 2006 |
| "financial freedom" | 8.8x growth | 2019 |
| "personal freedom" | 20% decline | 1971 |
| "political freedom" | 20% decline | 1971 |

As political freedom faded from books, financial freedom surged.

### The FIRE Movement

The rise of "financial freedom" tracks specific events:

- **1992**: *Your Money or Your Life* published
- **1997**: *Rich Dad Poor Dad* goes mainstream
- **2011**: Mr. Money Mustache blog launches
- **2018**: "Playing with FIRE" documentary

### Two Kinds of Economic Freedom

| Type | What It Means | Trend |
|------|---------------|-------|
| Financial freedom (personal) | Not having to work | Growing fast |
| Economic freedom (political) | Free markets vs. communism | Stagnant |

The personal framing is winning. Freedom increasingly means individual economic autonomy, not political systems.

## The 2020s: Freedom Fragmented

Because books take years to publish, they cannot capture the speed of modern language change. For the 2020s, we shifted our lens from printed text to real-time Google Search data to see how people use the word in the moment:

| Term | When Search Interest Peaked | Context |
|------|----------------|---------|
| "vaccine mandate" | November 2021 | Biden federal mandate |
| "freedom convoy" | February 2022 | Canadian trucker protests |
| "freedom of speech" | January 2021 | Social media bans |
| "medical freedom" | 2021-2022 | Anti-mandate movement |

### Competing Freedoms

Today, different groups use "freedom" to mean different things:

| Freedom Type | Who Uses It | What They Mean |
|--------------|-------------|----------------|
| Medical freedom | Right/libertarian | No vaccine mandates |
| Financial freedom | Apolitical | Not working for money |
| Freedom of speech | Right-leaning | No social media censorship |
| Religious freedom | Conservative | Church autonomy |

"Freedom" no longer unifies. It divides.

## Two Kinds of Freedom

In 1958, philosopher Isaiah Berlin identified two concepts that had gotten tangled together:

- **Negative Liberty**: Freedom FROM interference. The state leaves you alone.
- **Positive Liberty**: Freedom TO achieve self-mastery. The state helps you flourish.

Our data shows when Positive Liberty started winning in published text: gradually, between 1860 and 1920. This aligns with the rise of "New Liberalism" — T.H. Green, L.T. Hobhouse, and others who argued that true freedom required material conditions, and therefore state intervention.

We can't pin it to a single year. The shift shows up across decades, and its exact timing depends on how we measure it. But the direction is clear and consistent across methods.

### The Political Consequence

**Under the old definition** (negative liberty): My freedom doesn't subtract from yours. We can both be free.

**Under the new definition** (positive liberty): My "freedom" may require resources from you. If I'm too poor to exercise my freedom, the state must intervene—funded by your taxes.

| Freedom | Who Pays? |
|---------|-----------|
| "Freedom from slavery" | Slave owner loses power |
| "Freedom to vote" | No one loses |
| "Financial freedom" | The broader economy's labor generates your returns |
| "Medical freedom" | Others bear health risks if you refuse vaccines |

When "freedom" meant "not a slave," everyone could agree on what it meant. When "freedom" means "capacity to live as I choose," disagreement is inevitable—because your resources fund my freedom.

## Summary: The Arc of Freedom

### Before 1500
**Legal status.** Freedom = not being a slave. Concrete and binary.

### 1500-1700
**Religious meaning.** "God" was the #1 word near "freedom." 79% positive framing: freedom to worship, liberty to speak.

### 1800-1920
**Abolition era.** Freedom flipped to negative: "freedom from slavery." By 1870-1920, 76% of freedom language was about escaping constraints.

### 1965
**The crossover.** "Freedom to" passed "freedom from" in books for the first time since the 1600s.

### 1970-Today
**Financial turn.** Political freedom declined while "financial freedom" surged 8.8x.

### 2020s
**Fragmentation.** Multiple competing "freedoms" used by different groups. Modern freedoms increasingly imply trade-offs.

## How We Checked Our Work

### The divergence is real

We compared "freedom" and "liberty" against control word pairs—justice/fairness, truth/honesty, honor/dignity, power/authority, king/queen. Freedom/liberty diverged more than any of them. The average synonym pair actually *converged* slightly over the same period. Something specific happened to these two words.

### Freedom moved in a direction no other word did

We built a semantic axis running from "constraint" (slavery, bondage, oppression) to "agency" (autonomy, choice, capacity) and tracked how 11 abstract nouns moved along it.

Freedom was **the only word besides "democracy"** that moved toward the agency end. Liberty, justice, truth, honor—every other word drifted toward the constraint end. Whatever happened to "freedom" was specific to it, not a general trend in the language.

### The shift was gradual, not sudden

We originally reported a "crossover around 1880." Robustness testing showed the exact decade depends on how you define the categories. The more honest picture: freedom drifted steadily toward agency from 1800 to about 1920, then leveled off. It was a century-long process, not a single event.

The 1860-1920 window still aligns with the intellectual transition from Classical to New Liberalism—but it was a slow tide, not a wave.

## What We Couldn't Measure

- **1700-1800 gap**: No good data for the American and French Revolutions
- **American focus**: Most data is from American English
- **Published text bias**: Books and newspapers, not everyday speech
- **No partisan breakdown**: Didn't separate conservative vs. progressive usage
- **Crossover precision**: The legal-to-personal shift timing is approximate (1860-1920), not a single year

## The Bottom Line

Freedom hasn't drifted—it has swung.

From "not being a slave" to "liberty of conscience" to "freedom from oppression" to "financial freedom"—the word keeps moving.

The most striking pattern is the U-shape: positive in the 1500s, negative during abolition, positive again today. But modern positive freedom is structurally different from the old kind.

Between 1860 and 1920, intellectuals began redefining "freedom" from a legal status (you have rights) to a personal capacity (you can do what you want). This confusion—between liberty and ability—turned politics into a zero-sum game. The data shows when this redefinition took hold and spread through the educated class.

The word endures. Its capture by different factions continues.
